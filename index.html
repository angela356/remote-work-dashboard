Does remote work trade productivity against well-being?

Remote work surged during COVID and settled into a structural feature of European labour. The evidence here points to a nuanced answer: the well-being penalty of working from home is small and uneven — and on some measures, office-bound workers fare worse.

Key Findings — The Story in Six Charts

A guided tour through the central question: does working from home cost workers their well-being? The short answer the data gives is less than you would think — and the picture is shaped as much by who works remotely as by remote work itself.

Finding 1. Well-being didn't collapse with remote work — it dipped during the forced-isolation of 2021, then recovered to above its pre-pandemic level by 2024, even as remote work stayed common.

Remote Work in Europe: Productivity & Well-being

This dashboard explores the relationship between remote work adoption, worker well-being, and productivity across European countries, using a multi-source data approach combining macro-statistical trends with survey microdata.

Data Sources

  • Eurostat LFSA_EHOMP — Employed persons working from home by professional status, % of total employment. Annual Labour Force Survey, 2010–2025.
  • R-MAP Project Survey (Horizon Europe 101132497) — N=20,959 respondents across European countries. Perceptions, intentions, and mobility patterns related to remote work.
  • Eurofound EWCS 2015 — 6th European Working Conditions Survey. N=43,850 face-to-face interviews (CAPI). Pre-COVID baseline.
  • Eurofound EWCTS 2021 — European Working Conditions Telephone Survey. N=71,758 interviews. COVID-peak period.
  • Eurofound EWCS 2024 — 7th European Working Conditions Survey. N=36,644 mixed-mode interviews. Post-COVID landscape.
  • Post-Pandemic Remote Work Health Impact 2025 — N=3,157 respondents. Health and well-being outcomes by work arrangement.
  • Eurostat ICT Survey (isoc_ci_mvis, isoc_ci_ras) — enterprises conducting remote meetings & offering remote access, by size class and country, 2022 & 2024.
  • IoT Analytics “State of IoT” — global installed base of connected IoT devices (billions), 2015–2025.

    • Methodological Notes

    • WHO-5 Well-being Index: 0–100 scale, computed from five items. Scores below 50 indicate risk of depression (WHO threshold).
    • Productivity proxy: derived from Likert item "I feel less productive while working remotely" (R-MAP, 1–7 scale, inverted). Direct productivity measurement is not available in survey data.
    • WFH frequency harmonization: EWCS 2015 labels (Daily / Several times a week / etc.) mapped to unified 5-level scheme (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Always) shared with 2021 and 2024 waves.
    • Survey weights applied where available: w5 (EWCS 2015), weight_core (EWCTS 2021), calweight (EWCS 2024).
    • Family type: derived from EWCTS 2021 household composition — size combined with presence of children under 16 (Living alone / Couple, no children / Multi-adult, no children / Household with children).
    • Salary: Health Impact 2025 salary bands; averages computed using band midpoints ($50K–$130K).
    • Commute time saved: estimated as remote days × one-way commute × 2, from R-MAP self-reported commute minutes.
    • Aggregated data only: this dashboard displays pre-computed group means and percentages. No individual-level microdata is included.
